## How to Use This Document

### Layering Convention

**"A OVER B"** means:
- **A** = TOP layer (applied 2nd, on top)
- **B** = BASE layer (applied 1st, on bisque)

Example: "Celadon OVER Tenmoku" = Dip Tenmoku first → Dry → Dip Celadon second

### Application Methods

| Method | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Double-dip | Dip in glaze A, dry, dip in glaze B | Even coverage, production pieces |
| Brush-over | Brush glaze A, dry, brush glaze B | Controlled areas, decorative effects |
| Spray-over | Spray glaze A, dry, spray glaze B | Gradual transitions, large pieces |

## Key Chemistry Questions Answered

### Tom Coleman Clear vs Lucid Clear

| Property | Tom Coleman Clear (TC-103) | Lucid Clear (LG-27) |
|----------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **Contains Zinc** | YES | NO (zinc-free) |
| **Color Response** | Enhanced color pop with oxides | True-to-color, no shifting |
| **Chrome Greens** | Can affect chrome-based greens | Safe with all colorants |
| **Surface** | Can create matte effects with certain colorants | Pure transparent gloss |
| **Best Use** | Over slips, oxides, stains | Over underglazes, colored bases |

**When to use which:**
- **Tom Coleman Clear:** Want enhanced color intensity, especially with iron/copper oxides
- **Lucid Clear:** Want underglaze colors to remain true, no surprises

### Honey Luster vs Mellow Yellow

Both appear similar on test tiles. Differences likely include:

| Factor | Honey Luster | Mellow Yellow |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| **Surface** | Iridescent/metallic sheen | Solid matte or satin |
| **Luster Effect** | Visible under direct light | None |
| **Opacifier** | May use tin for luster | Likely zircopax |
| **Best Test** | Side-by-side under same raking light | - |

**Action:** Test both side-by-side under raking light to see luster effect.

### 1.1 Classic Eastern Combinations

#### Chun Blue OVER Tenmoku

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Application** | Tenmoku on rim first (thin) → Chun Blue over entire piece |
| **Result** | Dark brown/black base with violet edges where Chun is thin |
| **Chemistry Why** | Chun (copper + iron) over high-iron Tenmoku creates purple flash at boundary |
| **Best On** | Rims, bowls, vertical forms with thin edges |
| **Risk** | Run potential on vertical surfaces - watch thickness |
| **If Reversed (Tenmoku over Chun Blue)** | Tenmoku would cover/obscure the Chun Blue entirely (opaque dark glaze over translucent blue). No purple flashing. Tenmoku might crawl if applied too thick over glossy Chun surface. Less interesting result - just black glaze. |

#### Celadon OVER Malcom's Shino

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Application** | Shino base (even coat) → Celadon over (thin to medium) |
| **Result** | Orange/gray carbon trap shino with jade green pooling where celadon is thicker |
| **Chemistry Why** | Shino as base prevents crawling; celadon pools in shino texture, creating depth |
| **Best On** | Textured surfaces, carved decoration |
| **Risk** | Low when shino is base - this is the correct layering order |
| **If Reversed (Shino over Celadon)** | **CATASTROPHIC FAILURE.** Shino's high clay content + high surface tension + early soda ash melt = crawling on non-porous celadon surface. Bare patches, peeling, "disaster." See Section 11 for science. |

### 5.2 Firing Sequences

#### Standard Sequence
1. Bisque (electric, Cone 06)
2. Glaze fire (gas, Cone 10 reduction)

#### Refire Sequence
1. Bisque
2. Glaze fire
3. Evaluate
4. Refire if needed (same or lighter reduction)

### 5.3 Reduction Variability

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Body reduction timing | Affects clay color, flashing |
| Glaze reduction intensity | Affects copper reds, celadon greenness |
| Carbon trap window | 850°C+ for shinos |
| Final oxidation | Brighter surfaces vs reduced depth |

## Section 7: CLAY BODY SCIENCE - What the Specs Actually Mean

These numbers aren't just catalog trivia. They tell you how the clay will behave, what it's good for, and—importantly—how to break the rules if you want to.

### 7.1 Shrinkage

**What it is:** How much the clay shrinks from wet to fired.

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|--------------|
| Wet → Leatherhard | ~5% shrinkage (water leaves) |
| Leatherhard → Bisque | ~2-3% shrinkage (chemical water leaves) |
| Bisque → Cone 10 | ~5-8% shrinkage (vitrification) |

**Why it matters:**
- Sizing lids, fitting parts together
- Warpage prediction (higher shrinkage = more warpage risk)
- Glaze fit - clay and glaze must shrink at compatible rates

**Your clays ranked:**

| Clay | Shrinkage | Implication |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Babu | 15% | Highest - watch for warpage, lids need extra care |
| Most others | 12-12.5% | Standard range |
| B-Mix | 12% | Lower than Babu = more stable |

### 7.2 Absorption

**What it is:** How much water the fired clay absorbs when soaked. Measured by weighing dry, soaking 24 hours, weighing wet.

**What it tells you:**

| Absorption | Vitrification | Functional Use |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| **<1%** | Fully vitrified | Dinnerware, holds liquids |
| **1-2%** | Nearly vitrified | Good for most functional work |
| **2-5%** | Semi-vitreous | Decorative, may weep over time |
| **>5%** | Porous | Planters, sculptural only |

**Why you care:**
- **Dinnerware safety** - Low absorption = won't absorb bacteria, oils, dishwasher water
- **Freezing** - High absorption + freezing = cracking (water expands in pores)
- **Staining** - Coffee/tea stains porous clay from inside

**Your clays:**

| Clay | Absorption | Functional? |
|------|------------|-------------|
| Babu | 0.5% | Yes - fully vitrified |
| Death Valley | 0.8% | Yes - very dense |
| Dark Brown | 0.9% | Yes |
| B-Mix | 1.2% | Yes |
| Stoney White | 1.5% | Yes - still good for dinnerware |

All studio clays are dinnerware-safe at Cone 10. That's intentional - they're chosen for functional work.

### 7.3 COE (Coefficient of Thermal Expansion)

**What it is:** How much the clay expands when heated and contracts when cooling. Measured in parts per million per degree Celsius.

**Why it matters: GLAZE FIT**

```
Clay COE > Glaze COE = CRAZING (glaze cracks, too small for clay)
Clay COE < Glaze COE = SHIVERING (glaze pops off, too big for clay)
Clay COE ≈ Glaze COE = GOOD FIT
```

**The numbers:**

| Clay | COE | What It Means |
|------|-----|---------------|
| Stoney White | 5.39 | Lower - matches most glazes easily |
| Babu | 5.74 | Slightly higher |
| B-Mix 5 w/ Grog | 5.65 | Mid-range |

**Practical use:**
- Most studio glazes are formulated for COE 5.5-6.0
- If a glaze always crazes on one clay but not others, it's a COE mismatch
- You can't easily change clay COE, but you CAN adjust glaze (add silica to lower its COE)

**The dirty secret:** Most potters ignore COE until they have a problem. Then it becomes very important.

### 7.4 Sand/Grog Content

**What it is:** Particles added to the clay that don't shrink or melt.

**Why manufacturers add it:**

| Purpose | How It Works |
|---------|--------------|
| **Reduce shrinkage** | Grog doesn't shrink = less overall shrinkage |
| **Increase strength** | Particles act like aggregate in concrete |
| **Improve drying** | Creates pathways for water to escape |
| **Prevent cracking** | Interrupts clay particle alignment |
| **Add texture** | Visual and tactile effect |

**Why YOU care:**

| Sand/Grog Level | Best For | Hand Feel |
|-----------------|----------|-----------|
| **None (smooth)** | Delicate work, burnishing, fine details | Silk, no abrasion |
| **Fine (<60 mesh)** | General throwing, slight texture | Slight tooth |
| **Medium (30-60 mesh)** | Large pots, sculptural | Abrasive after long session |
| **Coarse (>30 mesh)** | Handbuilding, very large work | Very abrasive |

### 7.5 Other Clay Descriptors

#### Penetrometer Target
Measurement of clay stiffness/hardness when wet. Higher = firmer. Most of your clays are target 7 - standard throwing consistency.

#### Grolleg
A specific type of English kaolin known for exceptional whiteness and translucency. Babu is a "grolleg porcelain" = whiter and more translucent than regular porcelain.

#### Open Body
Clay formulated to remain slightly porous even at full temperature. Stoney White is "slightly open even at Cone 10" - more resistant to thermal shock (ovenware), slightly more absorbent.

#### Mottled Look (Reduction Effect)
How the clay surface varies in color due to uneven reduction. Stoney White = "gray-white mottled" from iron spots. B-Mix = more uniform (less iron).

## Section 8: BREAKING THE RULES - Experimental Manipulation

**The following experiments intentionally misuse, break, and exploit ceramic parameters.** These are not accidents—these are deliberate techniques for creating surfaces that "proper" ceramics education tells you to avoid.

**Warning labels are for people who want predictable results. You don't.**

### 8.1 Shrinkage Exploitation

#### Deliberate Warpage

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Uneven drying** | Dry one side faster (heat, fan, sun) | Controlled warping toward dry side |
| **Thickness variation** | Thin/thick areas in same piece | Thin warps more, creates organic curves |
| **Mixed clay bodies** | Join high-shrinkage (Babu 15%) with low-shrinkage (B-Mix 12%) | Internal stress = intentional cracking, splitting, pulling apart |
| **Wet additions** | Add wet clay to leatherhard piece | Added section shrinks more = deliberate separation/cracking |

**Experiment:** Make a cylinder from B-Mix, attach a Babu rim. Watch the rim shrink 3% more than the body during firing. The rim will either crack dramatically or create extreme compression patterns.

#### Compression Cracking

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Inward warping** | Force piece to warp inward during drying | Surface wrinkles, folds |
| **Multiple drying cycles** | Wet, partially dry, re-wet, dry again | Stress patterns, micro-cracking |

### 8.2 Absorption Exploitation

#### Deliberate Underfiring

| Technique | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| **Fire to Cone 8 instead of 10** | Clay remains porous (3-5% absorption instead of <2%) |
| **Post-fire staining** | Porous clay absorbs iron, oxides, stains INTO the body |
| **Wax/oil treatments** | Traditional Japanese tobegano (tea stains), linseed oil darkening |
| **Salt soaking** | Salt crystals form in pores = crystalline surface texture |

**Experiment:** Underfire Death Valley to Cone 8. Soak in iron sulfate solution. The porous clay drinks the iron, creating rust patterns deep in the body. Refire to Cone 6 to set.

#### Deliberate Overfiring (Bloating)

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Fire past maturity** | Cone 11-12 on Cone 10 clay | Gases trapped in body = internal bubbles = surface bloating |
| **Fast cooling** | Trap gases before they escape | Surface blisters, cratering |
| **Reduction bloating** | Heavy reduction + overfire | Black core, surface bubbling, metallic effects |

**Experiment:** Fire B-Mix to Cone 12 in heavy reduction. The clay will bloat from within, creating a surface that looks like lava or diseased skin. Beautiful for sculptural work. Useless for functional.

### 8.3 COE Mismatch Exploitation

#### Controlled Crazing

Crazing is a "defect" only if you don't want it. Deliberate crazing = crackle patterns.

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **High-COE glaze on low-COE clay** | Glaze shrinks more than clay | Fine crackle network |
| **Thick glaze application** | More glaze = more stress | Larger crackle patterns |
| **Thermal shock after firing** | Heat piece, quench in cold water | Instant crazing, audible cracking |
| **Raku crackle** | Remove from kiln hot, quench | Dramatic black crackle lines |

**Experiment:** Dip a bisque tile in a high-alkali glaze (high COE). Fire. The entire surface will craze. Ink the cracks with India ink, wipe surface. Now you have a black-line crackle pattern.

#### Deliberate Shivering

Shivering = glaze popping off. Usually a defect. Sometimes a texture.

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Low-COE glaze on high-COE clay** | Glaze shrinks less than clay | Glaze pops off in sheets, flakes |
| **Layer incompatible glazes** | Different COEs in layers | Top layer pops, reveals bottom |
| **Local stress points** | Thick/thin glaze transitions | Controlled shivering at edges |

**Experiment:** Apply a low-expansion matte glaze thick on Babu porcelain. Fire. The glaze may shiver off in patterns, revealing the white clay beneath. Control where it shivers by varying thickness.

### 8.4 Inclusion Manipulation

#### Foreign Inclusions

| Material | Effect | Risk Level |
|----------|--------|------------|
| **Steel wool** | Rust patterns, iron bleeding into glaze | Medium - rust expands |
| **Copper wire** | Green/blue halos around wire, local reduction | Low |
| **Nails/screws** | Rust spots, dramatic iron marks | Medium - expansion |
| **Glass beads** | Melt into pools, may crack during cooling | High - COE mismatch |
| **Sea shells** | Burn out, leave holes; calcium may flux local area | Low |
| **Rice/beans/seeds** | Burn out completely, organic-shaped voids | Low |
| **Coffee grounds** | Burn out, fine texture, slight carbon | Low |
| **Sawdust** | Burn out, texture, carbon trapping potential | Low |
| **Paper pulp** | Paper clay - extreme strength when wet, burns out | Low |

**Experiment:** Press steel wool into wet Death Valley clay. The iron in the wool creates localized rust spots that show through glaze. Each piece unique.

#### Flux-Bearing Inclusions

Some materials don't just sit there—they melt and actively affect surrounding clay/glaze.

| Material | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Frit chips** | Melt and pool, create glass spots |
| **Soda ash crystals** | Local fluxing, glaze texture |

**Experiment:** Throw a pot in B-Mix. Press wood ash into the surface in patterns. Fire. The ash will flux the clay surface locally, creating shiny spots and color variation without any applied glaze.

Materials that refuse to participate—and that refusal becomes the pattern.

| Material | Effect |
|----------|--------|

| **Wax inclusions** | Burn out, leave voids |
| **Plastic beads** | Melt and burn, create craters |

#### Laminated Clay Bodies

| **Incomplete joining** |故意 weak bonds | Pieces separate during firing |

### 8.6 Firing Manipulation

#### Reduction Timing Experiments

| When Reduction Happens | Effect |
|------------------------|--------|
| **Early (body reduction)** | Clay darkens, flashing develops, glazes affected less |
| **Late (glaze reduction)** | Glazes affected, clay stays lighter |
| **Throughout** | Maximum reduction effects |
| **Oscillating** | Reduction/oxidation/reduction = layered effects |

**Experiment:** Fire two identical Death Valley pieces with identical glazes. One: early heavy reduction. Two: late heavy reduction. Same clay, same glaze, completely different colors.

#### Atmosphere Corruption

| Atmosphere | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| **Heavy reduction** | Carbon trapping, dark colors, metallic effects |
| **Oxidation** | Bright colors, clean surfaces |
| **Neutral** | Somewhere between |
| **Salt/soda vapor** | Glaze forms on bare clay |
| **Wood firing** | Ash glaze, flashing,Fly ash marks |
| **Dirty kiln** | Reduction from previous firing residue |

**Experiment:** Place a bare Death Valley pot near the burner port. The direct flame will create flash marks. Salt the kiln shelves near your pot. Vapor glaze on bare clay.

#### Cooling Manipulation

| Cooling | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **Fast cool** | Freeze glaze in place, smaller crystals or none |
| **Slow cool** | Crystal growth (teadust, oil spot, crystalline) |
| **Hold at temperature** | Crystal development, glaze running |

| **Crash cool + hold** | Unusual crystal formation |

**Experiment:** Fire Teadust glaze. Cooling option A: fast cool. Option B: slow cool through 1800-1500°F. Option A = few/no crystals. Option B = dramatic crystal spotting.

#### The "Frankenpot"

### 8.10 The "Forbidden" Experiments

These are things that make ceramics teachers nervous. They're not actually dangerous if you understand what you're doing—they just violate the "functional pottery" mindset.

|------------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Raku functional ware** | Still porous, not food safe | Crackles, metallics, drama |

| **Fire found objects with clay** | Unknown materials | Happy accidents, serendipity |

## Section 10: SLIP EXPERIMENTATION

**Key Insight:** Slip is the intermediate layer between clay body and glaze. It's the most underexploited creative variable in most studios. This section is about using slip as an experimental surface—especially for organic, variegated effects that interact with atmosphere and glaze.

### 10.1 What is Slip?

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Slip** | Clay + water = cream to yogurt consistency |
| **Casting slip** | Deflocculated for fluidity with low water content |
| **Decorating slip** | Applied to surface for color/texture before glazing |
| **Engobe** | Slip with added colorants and/or fluxes |
| **Terra sigillata** | Ultra-fine slip, burnishable to sheen |

**Why slip matters for glaze layering:**
- Slip changes the surface chemistry glaze interacts with
- Slip can introduce color beneath transparent glazes
- Slip texture affects how glaze pools and breaks
- Slip shrinkage mismatch creates deliberate cracking patterns

### 10.2 Making Slip from Studio Clays

You have 8 clay bodies. Each can become slip.

#### Basic Process

```
1. Take dry clay (let a bag dry out, or use trimmings)
2. Break into small pieces
3. Add to water - let slake (dissolve) 24-48 hours
4. Mix thoroughly (blunger, mixer, or by hand)
5. Sieve through 80-100 mesh for smooth slip
6. Adjust consistency:
   - Too thick: Add water
   - Too thin: Let settle, pour off water
   - Need fluidity without adding water: Add deflocculant (sodium silicate)
```

#### Studio Slip Inventory

| Clay Body | Shrinkage | Slip Characteristics |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| B-Mix | 12% | Creamy white, good base for colorants |
| Death Valley | 12.5% | Rich iron content = warm terracotta slip |
| Babu | 15% | High-shrink white porcelain slip |
| Laguna Speckled Buff | 12% | Speckled brown slip |
| Laguna Brown | 11.5% | Dark brown slip |
| Laguna Dark Brown | 12% | Very dark slip |
| Laguna Rocky Mountain | 12% | Toasted buff slip |
| Long Beach Red | 13% | Red slip, high iron |

### 10.3 Slip Consistency Guide

| Use | Consistency | Test |
|-----|-------------|------|
| **Dipping** | Heavy cream | Coats evenly, drips off |
| **Brushing** | Yogurt | Stays on brush, flows off |
| **Trailing** | Soft butter | Holds shape, doesn't slump much |
| **Wash/thin** | Whole milk | Very thin, multiple coats needed |
| **Terra sig** | Skim milk | Ultra-thin, 10+ coats, burnishable |

### 10.4 Slip Over Different Bodies (Shrinkage Mismatch)

**This is where slip becomes experimental.**

| Slip Over Body | Differential | Predicted Effect | Creative Use |
|----------------|--------------|------------------|--------------|
| Babu slip (15%) over B-Mix (12%) | +3% | Slip shrinks more = potential cracking, compression patterns | Deliberate crackle patterns |
| B-Mix slip (12%) over Babu (15%) | -3% | Body shrinks more = slip may flake, separate | Distressed, aged surface |
| Death Valley slip (12.5%) over B-Mix (12%) | +0.5% | Minimal stress | Iron-rich surface over white body |
| Babu slip over Death Valley | +2.5% | White over dark, shrinkage stress | High contrast, potential crackle |
| Same-body slip over body | 0% | No stress, smooth integration | Clean base for colorants |

**Key Question:** Can shrinkage mismatch be exploited for deliberate cracking/patterning? **Yes.**

**Experiment:** Apply thick Babu slip over Death Valley body. Force dry quickly with heat gun. Slip will crack in a network pattern. Fire with clear glaze — cracks become dark lines where glaze pools.

### 10.7 Organic Slip Colorants (Not Flat Mason Stains)

**The goal:** Alive, variegated surfaces that respond to firing atmosphere. NOT flat, commercial-looking color.

| Material | Amount (dry weight %) | Effect | Why Better Than Stains |
|----------|----------------------|--------|------------------------|
| **Iron oxide** | 5-15% | Rust, orange, brown bleeding | Natural, variegated, atmosphere-sensitive |
| **Copper carbonate** | 2-8% | Green/blue flashing, reduction-sensitive | Alive, varies with atmosphere, halos |
| **Cobalt carbonate** | 1-3% | Blue with halo effects | Softer edges than stain |
| **Rutile** | 5-15% | Tan with crystal spots | Complex, not flat |
| **Wood ash** | 10-30% | Local fluxing, color variation | Unpredictable, organic glossy spots |
| **Bone ash** | 5-10% | Opalescent, subtle white | Complex surface, responds to atmosphere |
| **Granite dust** | 10-20% | Feldspar melts locally | Speckled gloss, texture |
| **Copper wire pieces** | Embedded | Flashing halos, green/blue bleed | Very organic, line-based |
| **Steel wool bits** | Embedded | Rust spots, iron bleeding | Industrial, alive |
| **Salt crystals** | Sprinkled | Local pitting and fluxing | Texture, gloss variations |

**Key Principle:** These materials interact with firing atmosphere, each other, and the glaze layer. Mason stains are inert — they just sit there looking flat.

#### Mixing Colorants into Slip

```
1. Weigh dry clay material
2. Add colorant as % of dry clay weight
3. Mix colorant into dry clay before adding water
   OR dissolve colorant in water first, then add clay
4. Add water and mix thoroughly
5. Sieve for even distribution
```

**Example:** 500g dry clay + 50g iron oxide (10%) = iron slip

### 10.11 Terra Sigillata

**What it is:** Ultra-fine slip particles, deflocculated and settled. Creates ultra-smooth surface that can be burnished to sheen.

**Historical context:** Used in classical Roman pottery, Native American pottery for waterproof, polished surfaces.

**Making terra sig:**

```
1. Make slip from clay body
2. Add deflocculant (sodium silicate, 0.5%)
3. Let settle 24-48 hours
4. Three layers form:
   - Top: water
   - Middle: ultra-fine particles (terra sig)
   - Bottom: coarse particles
5. Siphon off middle layer = terra sigillata
```

**Application:**

| Property | Method |
|----------|--------|
| Consistency | Like whole milk or thinner |
| Coats | 5-10+ thin coats |
| Between coats | Let dry, burnish gently |
| Final | Burnish to sheen with stone or back of spoon |
| Firing | Low fire best (Cone 06-04) — higher fires lose sheen |

**In reduction at Cone 10:** Terra sig loses burnish but gains atmosphere response. Iron-bearing terra sig will flash and variegated.

## Section 11: GLAZE LAYERING RULES - The Technical Science

This section covers the official technical rules for layering glazes, distinguishing between myths, misconceptions, and proven ceramic science.

 Special attention to **shino glazes** due to their unique behavior.


### 11.1 General Layering Principles (All Glazes)

| Principle | Explanation | Rule |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| **Thermal Expansion (COE)** | Glaze and clay must expand/contract at similar rates during cooling | Glaze COE slightly lower than body COE = slight compression = good fit |
| **Viscosity & Melt Fluidity** | Fluid glazes flow and run; non-fluid glazes stay put | Fluid glazes can be applied thicker over non-fluid bases |
 **Surface Tension** | Some glazes pull away from underlying layers during melting | High surface tension glazes risk as base layers |
 **Drying Shrinkage** | High-clay glazes shrink dramatically as drying | High shrinkage = stress on underlying layers |

### 11.2 The "Which Goes First?" Decision Framework

| Factor | Put This Layer FIRST | Why |
|--------|---------------------|-----|
| **Higher clay content** | ✓ Goes first (base) | High shrinkage creates stress on over-layers |
| **Higher surface tension** | ✓ Goes first (base) | Pulls away from underlying surfaces |
| **Higher viscosity** | ✓ Goes first (base) | Won't flow to heal cracks |
| **Lower melting point** | ✓ Goes first (base) | Early melting seals movement stresses under-layers |
| **Contains soluble fluxes** | ✓ Goes first (base) | Flux migration affects underlying layers |

**General Rule:** The more "difficult" a glaze (high clay, high shrinkage, high surface tension), the earlier it must be applied.

### 11.3 SHINO LAYERING - The Definitive Rules

#### The Core Rule: Shino First

**"Shino first or suffer the curse"** - This is NOT a myth. It's backed by materials science.

| Property | Shino Value | Effect |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Clay content** | High (>10%) | Dramatic drying shrinkage |
| **Surface tension** | Very high | Pulls away from underlying surfaces |
| **Soda ash** | 2-10% typical | Early melting at 1564°F (851°C) |
| **Alumina** | High | Creates viscous (non-fluid) melt |
| **Viscosity** | High (doesn't run) | Cannot flow to self-heal |

#### Why Shinos Must Be the Base Layer

When shino is applied OVER another glaze:

1. **High drying shrinkage** → Cracks form in the shino layer as it dries
2. **High surface tension** → Shino pulls away from the underlying glaze surface
3. **Early melting (soda ash)** → Shino begins melting while underlying glaze is still solid
4. **Viscous melt** → Cannot flow to heal the cracks
5. **Result:** Crawling, bare patches, peeling, "disaster"

#### What Happens When You Violate the Rule

| Violation | Mechanism | Result |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Shino OVER celadon/temmoku | Shrinkage + surface tension + incompatible thermal expansion | **Crawling** - "disaster" |
| Shino OVER any glossy glaze | Same mechanism | **Failure** - bare patches, peeling |
| Shino OVER non-gummed glaze | Fragile bisque bond + second layer stress | **Peeling, cracking** |

**Source:** Dave Finkelnburg (Ceramic Engineer, Alfred University) - "Putting shino over other glazes produces a disaster"
 (Ceramic Arts Network, Techno File: The Shino Myth)
 2020)

### 11.4 Traditional vs. Commercial Shinos - CRITICAL DISTINCTION

**IMPORTANT:** Commercial cone 5-6 shinos (like Coyote, AMACO) have DIFFERENT CHEMISTRY than traditional cone 9-10 reduction shinos. They may follow OP OP OP rules that are the OPPOSITE of the "shino first" rule.

#### Traditional Shinos (Cone 9-10 Reduction)

| Characteristic | Value | Why "Shino First" Applies |
|---------------|-------|----------------------------|
| Clay content | >10% (very high) | High shrinkage = must be base |
| Soda ash | 2-10% | Early melting at 1564°F |
| Surface tension | Very high | Pulls from underlying layers |
| Firing | Reduction, cone 9-10 | Complex carbon trapping |

**Layering Rule:** **Shino FIRST** (base layer only). Never apply shino over other glazes.

#### Commercial Cone 6 Shinos (Coyote, AMACO, etc.)

| Characteristic | Value | Effect on Layering |
|---------------|-------|-------------------|
| Clay content | Lower than traditional | Less shrinkage stress |
| Additives | Gums/binders for layer adhesion | Better bond between layers |
| Firing | Oxidation, cone 5-6 | No carbon trapping behavior |
| Melt behavior | More stable | Layering more flexible |

**Manufacturer Layering Guidance:**

| Manufacturer | Layering Order | Notes |
|--------------|----------------|-------|
| **Coyote Clay** | Shino **UNDER OR OVER** | "Shino glazes can be layered under or over nearly any of our glazes" |
| **AMACO** | Shino **FIRST** (base), other glaze second | "Apply 3 coats of base glaze (shino), then 3 coats of top glaze" |

**⚠️ WARNING:** Even commercial shinos can crawl! Always test tiles first. Thicker application = more crawling risk.

### 11.6 The "Why" Behind the Rules (Ceramic Science)

#### Drying Shrinkage Physics

When a glaze with high clay content dries:
1. Water evaporates → clay particles compact
2. Clay shrinks as it compacts → creates internal stress
3. If glaze is on bisque: stress absorbed by porous clay body
4. If glaze is on another glaze: **non-porous surface = stress causes cracking**

#### Surface Tension Mechanics

During firing, the glaze becomes liquid:
1. All liquids have surface tension (molecules pull toward each other)
2. High-clay, high-alumina glazes = **very high surface tension**
3. Surface tension causes the liquid to "ball up" rather than spread
4. If underlying surface is non-porous (already glazed): **glaze pulls away** = crawling

#### Early Melting (Soda Ash Effect)

Soda ash (sodium carbonate) in shinos:
- Melts at 1564°F (851°C) - **much lower** than most glaze components
- Creates early glaze movement while other glazes are still solid
- Movement on a solid surface = **disruption, stress, crawling**

### 11.7 Workarounds (If You Must Break Rules)

| Technique | Risk Level | How It Works |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **Apply shino VERY thin** | Medium | Less shrinkage = less stress |
| **Add CMC gum to underlying glaze** | Medium | Better adhesion to bisque |
| **Spray application** | Medium | Thinner, more even coat |
| **Single-fire (raw glaze on greenware)** | High | Different dynamics, unpredictable |

**Honest Assessment:** There is no reliable way to put traditional shino over other glazes. The chemistry doesn't allow it. Even workarounds have high failure rates.

### 11.8 What CAN Work Over Shino (Shino as Base)

| Glaze Type | Effect | Notes |
|------------|--------|-------|
| **Celadon over shino** | Shino shows through, carbon trapping visible | Beautiful, proven |
| **Temmoku over shino** | Dark pooling, shino breaks at edges | High contrast |
| **Clear over shino** | Preserves shino surface, adds depth | Protects crawling shino texture |
| **Washes over shino** | Subtle color modification | Shino character remains |

**Layering Rule:** Shino as BASE = you CAN layer other glazes ON TOP of shino. This works beautifully.

### 11.9 Test Protocol for Any Layering

Before testing ANY new glaze combination:

1. **Check this section** - Is there a specific rule for these glazes?
2. **Assess clay content** - Higher clay content = apply first
3. **Compare melting points** - Lower melting point = apply first
4. **Evaluate surface tension** - Higher surface tension = apply first
5. **Check manufacturer guidelines** - For commercial glazes
6. **Make test tiles** - ALWAYS test before production pieces
7. **Document results** - Record what worked and what didn't

### 11.10 Quick Reference: Shino Layering

| Rule | Traditional Shino | Commercial Shino |
|------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Layering Order** | **Shino FIRST** (base layer only) | Check manufacturer - varies |
| **Why** | High clay, high shrinkage, early melt | Modified chemistry, binders, lower shrinkage |
| **Shino over other glazes?** | **NO** - Catastrophic failure | Sometimes YES (Coyote, AMACO vary) |
| **Other glazes over shino?** | **YES** - Works beautifully | YES - Generally works |
| **What happens if violated** | Crawling, peeling, "disaster" | Possible crawling, test first |

### 11.11 Other Glaze Types With Layering Rules

Shinos aren't the only glazes with strict layering requirements. Here are other glaze types that have hard rules:

#### Crawling Glazes (White Crawl, etc.)

| Property | Value | Effect on Layering |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| **Purpose** | Designed to crawl intentionally | Creates textured, broken surface |
| **Clay content** | Typically high | Shrinks during drying |
| **Surface chemistry** | Modified to reduce adhesion | Deliberate crawling |

**Layering Rule:** Crawling glazes should be **BASE layer on bisque**. If applied over another glaze:
- May not crawl properly (the crawling requires the porous bisque surface)
- May not adhere (poor bond with non-porous glaze surface)
- Crawling becomes unpredictable

**Best Practice:** Apply crawling glaze directly to bisque, then layer OTHER glazes on top where you want to fill the crawled texture.

**Studio Glazes:** White Crawl

#### Slips and Engobes

| Property | Value | Effect on Layering |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| **Clay content** | Very high (mostly clay) | Extreme drying shrinkage |
| **Surface needed** | Porous (bisque or greenware) | Must bond to clay body |
| **Contains fluxes** | Engobes = yes, Slips = minimal | Different melt behavior |

**Layering Rule:** Slips and engobes must go **DIRECTLY on clay body** (bisque or greenware), **NEVER over other glazes**.

**Why:** They need the porous surface to adhere. Applied over a non-porous glaze surface, they will crack, flake, or peel off during drying or firing.

**Best Practice:**
1. Apply slip/engobe to bisque or leatherhard clay
2. Let dry completely
3. Apply glaze over the slip

#### Underglazes

| Property | Value | Effect on Layering |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| **Composition** | Clay + stain pigments | Minimal flux, doesn't melt |
| **Purpose** | Decorative color layer | Designed to stay put |
| **Surface needed** | Porous bisque or greenware | Good adhesion |

**Layering Rule:** Underglazes go **UNDER glazes** (hence the name), then covered with clear or transparent glaze.

**Why:** Underglazes don't form a glassy surface on their own. They need a glaze over them for:
- Durability (sealed surface)
- Gloss finish
- Food-safe functional ware

**Best Practice:**
1. Apply underglaze to bisque (or greenware)
2. Let dry completely
3. Apply clear or transparent glaze over
4. Fire

#### High-Clay Content Glazes (General Rule)

**Source:** Gabriel Kline, *Amazing Glaze Recipes and Combinations*

| Situation | Problem | Solution |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| High-clay glaze OVER low-clay glaze | Top layer shrinks more → flakes off | **Reverse order** or add CMC gum |
| High-clay glaze on bisque | Shrinks into porous surface → adheres | **Correct application** |

**General Rule:** The glaze with **higher clay content should be applied FIRST** (as base layer).

**Workarounds if you MUST reverse order:**
- Add 1% CMC gum to low-clay glaze (improves adhesion)
- Add 2% bentonite clay to low-clay glaze
- Apply high-clay glaze VERY thin

#### Quick Reference: Layering Order by Clay Content

| Apply FIRST (Base) | Apply SECOND (Top) | If Reversed (Top layer on bottom) |
|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Shinos | Celadons, clears | **Catastrophic crawling** - shino pulls away from non-porous surface |
| Crawling glazes | Fluid glazes | Crawling glaze may not crawl, poor adhesion on non-porous surface |
| Slips/engobes | Any glaze | **Flaking, peeling** - slip cannot bond to glazed surface |
| Underglazes | Clear glaze | Underglaze will not be sealed, not food-safe, may rub off |
| High-clay glazes | Low-clay glazes | High-clay layer shrinks more → flakes off low-clay base |

**Principle:** Higher clay content = more shrinkage = must go on porous surface = base layer.

#### Glaze Types That CAN Go Over Other Glazes

| Glaze Type | Why It Works Over Other Glazes |
|------------|-------------------------------|
| **Clear glazes** | Low clay content, fluid melt, good adhesion |
| **Celadons** | Low clay content, fluid, compatible with most surfaces |
| **Fluid gloss glazes** | Flow to self-heal, good surface bonding |
| **Washes** | Very thin, minimal stress on underlying layer |
